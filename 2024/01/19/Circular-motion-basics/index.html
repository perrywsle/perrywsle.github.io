Polar coordinates


The basis vectors of polar coordinates are represented as

r^andθ^0≤r≤∞0≤θ≤2π\begin{aligned} \hat{r} \hspace{0.1in} \mathrm{and} \hspace{0.1in}\hat\theta \\ \\ 0 ≤ r ≤ ∞ \\ 0 ≤ θ ≤ 2π \end{aligned}r^andθ^0≤r≤∞0≤θ≤2π​


r-hat and θ-hat are changing along with the coordinates, thus is represented as a function, in terms of i-hat, j-hat and θ (note: basis vectors have magnitude of 1):

Position vector

From the graph we know that the r and the theta are time dependent, thus the position vector, can be represented a length, r, times the direction it is pointing towards, which is r-hat:

r⃗(t)=rr^(t)=rcos⁡θ(t)ı^+rsin⁡θ(t)ȷ^\begin{aligned} \\& \vec{r}(t) \\&= r \hspace{0.05in} \hat{r}(t) \\& = r\cos\theta(t) \hspace{0.05in} \hat\imath + r\sin\theta(t) \hspace{0.05in} \hat\jmath \end{aligned}​r(t)=rr^(t)=rcosθ(t)ı^+rsinθ(t)ȷ^​​

The tangential unit vector isn’t included because it is perpendicular to the vector and doesn’t contribute to the vector component. Imagine a vector in a normal Cartesian plane pointing upwards, perpendicular to the x-axis: that vector’s i-hat component is zero. Same concept applies here.

It is derived that the velocity is tangential in direction of theta-hat (tangential).

Angular velocity

But there is another direction of velocity which is perpendicular to the direction of rotation (in or out of the plane), known as angular velocity:

ω⃗=dθdtk^\vec\omega=\frac{\mathrm{d}\theta}{\mathrm{d}t}\hspace{0.05in}\hat{k} ω=dtdθ​k^

dθdt=ωz\frac{\mathrm{d}\theta}{\mathrm{d}t}=\omega_z dtdθ​=ωz​

The k-hat defines if the angular velocity is pointing out of the plan (⊙), or into the plane (⊗). The direction can be defined using right-hand rule.

Angular velocity will have units of radians/s, and angular acceleration will have units of radians/s2. If we multiply these by a distance, such as r, the units will be m, m/s, or m/s2.
linear velocity (v) is equal to the product of the radial distance ® and the angular velocity (ω)

Angular frequency

It is essentially the same concept as angular velocity (rad/s), but it is used for waves and oscillations while angular velocity is used for circular motions. It is also denoted as ω:

ω=2πT=2πf\omega=\frac{2\pi}{T}=2\pi f ω=T2π​=2πf
