First and foremost, the fundamental concept, which is vector, must be clear.

Vector

  • Lines with magnitude and direction (arrow) in space
  • Where the magnitude and direction can be represented with matrixes
    E.g. (2D space)

[xy]\begin{bmatrix}x\\ y \end{bmatrix}[xy​]

x represents the steps taken along x-axis and y represents steps taken along y-axis
And so on: (3D space)

[xyz]\begin{bmatrix} x\\ y\\ z \end{bmatrix}⎣⎢⎡​xyz​⎦⎥⎤​

The principle of vectors’ addition and simple multiplication with constant (scalar multiplication) are straightforward, just motions added together and scaled, thus omitted.

Span

The set of all linear combination (column space) of given vectors.

av→+bw→,a,b∈Ra\overrightarrow{v} + b\overrightarrow{w}, \hspace{0.5in} a,b \in \mathbb{R} av+bw,a,b∈R

When there are multiple vectors, and one can be removed without reducing the span, the vectors are called linearly dependent, one of the vecotr can be expressed as the scaled/transformed version of the other.
When each vectors among a set of vetors all increases/decreases the span when added/removed, they are called linearly independent.

Basis vectors

A set of linearly independent vectors.
Usually represented as $$\hat\imath \hspace{0.1in} and \hspace{0.1in}\hat\jmath$$

Linear transformation

Concept

Basically pass input vector into some functions to produce output vector ¯_(ツ)_/¯, by transforming (shaping) the space grids
Characteristics of linear transformation:

  • lines remain straight
  • origin stays fixed
  • grid lines remain parallel and evenly spaced
  • preserve additivity and scaling

The (2D) transformation can be described in 2x2 matrix:

[xı^xȷ^yı^yȷ^]\begin{bmatrix} x_{\hat\imath} & x_{\hat\jmath}\\ y_{\hat\imath} & y_{\hat\jmath} \end{bmatrix} [xı^​yı^​​xȷ^​​yȷ^​​​]

Notable that the matrix is defined by place of î and ĵ after the transformation.

Applying the transformation to a vector:

[abcd][xy]=x[ac]+y[bd]=[ax+bycx+dy]\begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x\\ y \end{bmatrix}=x\begin{bmatrix} a\\ c \end{bmatrix}+y\begin{bmatrix} b\\ d \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} ax+by\\ cx+dy \end{bmatrix}[ac​bd​][xy​]=x[ac​]+y[bd​]=[ax+bycx+dy​]

Notice that it is just multiplying vector by the place where î and ĵ lands, according to x and y-axis respectively.

With that concept in mind, the 3D linear transformation would look like this:

[xi^xj^xk^yi^yj^yk^zi^zj^zk^][xyz]\begin{bmatrix} x_{\widehat{i}} & x_{\widehat{j}} & x_{\widehat{k}}\\ y_{\widehat{i}} & y_{\widehat{j}} & y_{\widehat{k}}\\ z_{\widehat{i}} & z_{\widehat{j}} & z_{\widehat{k}} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x\\ y\\ z \end{bmatrix}⎣⎢⎡​xi​yi​zi​​xj​​yj​​zj​​​xk​yk​zk​​⎦⎥⎤​⎣⎢⎡​xyz​⎦⎥⎤​

Cool stuff: when multiplying a vector by a linearly dependent column, the span dimension will be reduced (2D to 1D). This is called rank 1 tranformation.
Note: rank is the dimension of tranformation output, rank 1 for 1D and rank 2 for 2D and so on. Full rank means the dimesion is unchanged, lowered rank comes with null space or kernel (lines of vectors squished onto the origin).

Combining matrixes

To combine and sum up transformation, we multiply them, where order of transformation is arranged from right to left, because the right transformation occurred first, then the î and ĵ are transformed again, with a matrix multiplied on its left side:

[abcd][efgh]=[abcd][eg]+[abcd][fh]=[ae+bgaf+bhce+dgcf+dh]\begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} e & f\\ g & h \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} e\\ g \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} f\\ h \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} ae+bg & af+bh\\ ce+dg & cf+dh \end{bmatrix}[ac​bd​][eg​fh​]=[ac​bd​][eg​]+[ac​bd​][fh​]=[ae+bgce+dg​af+bhcf+dh​]

Changing basis vectors

Imagine a different coordinate system with a-hat and b-hat as its basis vectors, which when compared to the standard coordinate system would be some random vector unaligned with î and ĵ. To translate a certain vector from our coordinate system to their coordinate system would look like this:

[abcd]−1v→\begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}^{-1}\overrightarrow{v} [ac​bd​]−1v

It is just the inverse of basis matrix transformation (from ours to theirs inversed become theirs to ours) applied onto the vector (which is expressed in our coordinate system). This transforms the grids space from theirs to ours, and the vector expressed in our language to theirs. This is because the original basis vectors transformation matrix represents their basis vectors being translated to our language. Thus when inversed, it becomes the opposite, expressing our vectors in their language. Similarly to see how a vector written in their language in represented in our language, we apply the non-inversed basis vector transformation matrix to their language. A bit confusing yes.
Translation of transformation (from our language to theirs):

[acbd][prqs][acbd]−1\begin{bmatrix} a & c\\ b & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p & r\\ q & s \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a & c\\ b & d \end{bmatrix}^{-1} [ab​cd​][pq​rs​][ab​cd​]−1

Dot products

[ab]•[cd]=ac+bd\begin{bmatrix} a\\ b \end{bmatrix} • \begin{bmatrix} c\\ d \end{bmatrix} =a c + b d [ab​]•[cd​]=ac+bd

Really cool stuff going on involving duality, hard to explain by words so here is a link to 3blue1brown’s video:
From my understanding, it is the product of length of projected vector A onto vector B times length of vector B. It is somehow the same as 2x1 matrix transformation where the rank 1 transformation takes place (2D to 1D). This interchangeability is due to the fact that the basis vectors, when transformed via the 2x1 matrix, results in coordinates same as the vector_1 on the 1D number line they were projected to. This means the transformation on vector_2 (by multiplying by the 2x1 matrix) would result in the projection of it on the 1D number line that vector_1 is on. Therefore the second way of interpreting the dot product came out to be interpreting one of the vector as a (2x1) matrix although it is just 1x2, because of the correspondence characteristic of it. The computational result of matrix-vector product and dot product happens to be the same!

[ab][cd]=ac+bd\begin{bmatrix} a & b \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c\\ d \end{bmatrix} =a c + b d [a​b​][cd​]=ac+bd

[ab]•[cd]⇔[ab][cd]\begin{bmatrix} a\\ b \end{bmatrix} • \begin{bmatrix} c\\ d \end{bmatrix} \hspace{0.3in}\Leftrightarrow \hspace{0.3in} \begin{bmatrix} a & b \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c\\ d \end{bmatrix} [ab​]•[cd​]⇔[a​b​][cd​]

Dot product is useful in testing if the vectors are in similar directions or not (by looking at the positive negative value).

Cross product

The cross product is technically a new vector in a 3D space, where the length of the vector is equal to the determinant of the vectors.

[ac]×[bd]=det([abcd])\begin{bmatrix} a\\ c \end{bmatrix}\times \begin{bmatrix} b\\ d \end{bmatrix}=\mathrm{det}( \begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}) [ac​]×[bd​]=det([ac​bd​])

Note: it is important to think of the product as a vector and not parallelepiped volume. For deeper understanding, watch another 3blue1brown video:
From my understanding, we are basically finding the dual vector that when transforming with another given vector (3x1), gives the same result as the determinant of the given vector with another two constant vectors. The dual vector concept comes from the duality mentioned in dot products, where in this case is interpreted as the “dual vector” we are finding projected onto a line perpendicular to the parallelogram plane formed by the other two constant vectors. Notational trick is also taught in the video but I want to understand it geometrically (which I’m still trying to).

Cramer’s Rule

This is to determine the original vector when given a transformed vector along with its transformation matrix (A). Imagine two parallelograms, formed by î and ĵ vectors with a same vector X respectively. Their areas would be:

withı^:1×y=ywithȷ^:1×x=x\begin{aligned}\mathrm{with \hspace{0.1in}\hat\imath:\hspace{0.2in}}1\times y=y \\ \mathrm{with \hspace{0.1in}\hat\jmath:\hspace{0.2in}}1\times x=x \\ \end{aligned} withı^:1×y=ywithȷ^​:1×x=x​

After the transformation, the parallelogram areas will be scaled up:

Areaaftertransfomration(ı^)=y×det(A)Areaaftertransfomration(ȷ^)=x×det(A)\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Area \hspace{0.05in}after\hspace{0.05in}transfomration\hspace{0.05in}(\hat\imath)}=y\times \mathrm{det(A)} \\ \mathrm{Area \hspace{0.05in}after\hspace{0.05in}transfomration\hspace{0.05in}(\hat\jmath)}=x\times \mathrm{det(A)}\end{aligned} Areaaftertransfomration(ı^)=y×det(A)Areaaftertransfomration(ȷ^​)=x×det(A)​

Thus derived:

x/yori=Areaaftertransformationdet(A)x/y_{ori}=\frac{\mathrm{Area\hspace{0.05in}after\hspace{0.05in}transformation}}{\mathrm{det(A)}} x/yori​=det(A)Areaaftertransformation​

The area after the transformation can be found by arranging a 2x2 matrix with the transformed vector X with the corresponding basis vector transformation, then finding its determinant. This same concept applies to higher rank transformations, just with volumes and 3x3 matrixes and extra basis vectors 👍

Eigenvectors

Eigenvectors are vectors that remain on the same line of span after a linear transformation. To find eigenvectors, we first find the eigenvalues, which we can do by arranging the following equation:

Av→=λv→(A−λ)v→=0λ=[λ00λ]\begin{aligned} \mathrm{A}\overrightarrow{v}=\lambda\overrightarrow{v} \\(A-\lambda)\overrightarrow{v}=0 \\ \lambda= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned} Av=λv(A−λ)v=0λ=[λ0​0λ​]​

A represents linear transformation matrix, lambda represents eigenvalues, which is the scalar of the vector after linear transformation. This is true because for eigenvectors, the before and after are of the same except scaled differently. So solving for det(A-λ)=0 we can find the eigenvalues 👍This is because we know that the only when the transformation is lowered in rank (determinant = 0), then the result of transforming a non-zero vector would be zero.
To find the eigenvector, simply sub the λ back:

[a−λbcd−λ][xy]=[00]\begin{bmatrix} a-\lambda & b\\ c & d-\lambda \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} [a−λc​bd−λ​][xy​]=[00​]

Shortcut for computing eigenvalues

[abcd]λ1,λ2=m±m2−pm=a+d2p=ad−bc\begin{aligned}&\begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\\& \lambda_1,\hspace{0.02in}\lambda_2 = m ± \sqrt{m^2 - p}\\& m= \frac{a+d}{2} \\& p=ad-bc\end{aligned} ​[ac​bd​]λ1​,λ2​=m±m2−p​m=2a+d​p=ad−bc​

Eigenbasis

After finding out the eigenvectors, we can form a change of basis matrix. The reason to do so is because the math operations with eigenbasis can be a lot more easier, since they always come in such form:

[a00b]\begin{bmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & b \end{bmatrix} [a0​0b​]

Then we just translate our want-to-be-computed vectors to this eigenbasis language, compute it, then translate back again.
